DROUGHT

It is a natural disaster that occurs when there is a prolonged period of abnormally dry weather, resulting in a shortage of water for crops, animals, and people. Droughts can last for months or even years.They can occur anywhere in the world, but are most common in arid and semi-arid regions, and can have far-reaching impacts on ecosystems and societies, particularly in developing countries.

Understanding Drought

  • To better understand the science behind drought, scientists use a range of tools and techniques to monitor and predict drought conditions. For example, remote sensing technologies such as satellites can be used to track changes in vegetation cover and soil moisture, which can provide early warning of potential drought conditions. Additionally, climate models can be used to forecast future changes in weather patterns, which can help policymakers and communities better prepare for and adapt to droughts. Overall, the science of drought is a complex and multidisciplinary field that requires a deep understanding of meteorology, climatology, ecology, and other related disciplines. By better understanding the causes and impacts of drought, we can develop more effective strategies.

Causes of Drought

    Drought occurs due to many natural and human factors. Natural factors include:
  • Lack of precipitation: Drought occurs when there is little or no precipitation for an extended period of time.
  • Climate change: Climate change is causing increasingly severe weather conditions around the world.
  • Soil Moisture: Dry soil reduces evaporation, which reduces precipitation.
    • Anthropogenic factors causing the crisis include:
  • Deforestation: Trees play an important role in maintaining the water cycle. Deforestation disrupts the water cycle, causing drought.
  • Excessive use of water: Excessive use of water, such as groundwater, can lead to water shortages, leading to drought.

Impact of Drought

    Drought has significant social, economic and environmental impacts. Some of the effects of drought are:
  • Agriculture: Precipitation affects crop and animal production, causing food shortages and rising food prices.
  • Water Supply: Drought causes water shortages that affect families, businesses and ecosystems.
  • Environment: Climate change can cause forest fires, soil erosion and loss of biodiversity
  • Forest fires: Droughts can increase the risk of forest fires, as dry conditions make it easier for fires to spread.
  • Economic losses: Droughts can have significant economic impacts, as reduced agricultural productivity and water scarcity can lead to job losses and reduced economic growth.

Types of Droughts

Research in the early 1980s revealed more than 150 definitions of drought. Definitions reflect differences in areas, needs and regulatory approaches.

Wilhite and Glantz1 categorized definitions according to four basic approaches to drought measurement: meteorology, hydrology, agriculture, and socioeconomics. The first three approaches deal with how to measure drought as a physical phenomenon. The final section looks at drought in terms of supply and demand and traces the effects of water scarcity on socio-economic systems.

Meteorological drought

Meteorological drought is generally defined as the degree of drought (compared to some "normal" or average value) and the duration of the dry period. The definition of climatic drought should be considered region-specific, as the atmospheric conditions that cause precipitation differ from region to region.

Agricultural drought

Agricultural drought can be attributed to precipitation, difference between actual and potential evapotranspiration, soil moisture deficit, water base or low water reservoir etc. It links many aspects of meteorological (or hydrological) drought to agriculture. Plant water needs depend on weather conditions, the biology of a particular plant, its growth stage, and the physical and biological properties of the soil. A good definition of agricultural drought should take into account differences in crops at different stages of development from emergence to maturity. Insufficient moisture of the plant can affect germination, resulting in reduced crops per hectare and consequently low yields. However, if the soil moisture is sufficient for early growth, the lack of subsoil moisture at the initial stage will not affect the crop season as plants need water, or the end of the crop if soil moisture is added to the rain.

Hydrological drought

Hydrological drought is associated with the effect of insufficient precipitation (such as snow) on surface or underground water resources (such as rivers, streams and lakes, groundwater). The frequency and severity of hydrological drought is usually defined at the basin or watershed scale. While all droughts are caused by a lack of precipitation, hydrologists are more concerned with how a lack of precipitation works through water use. Hydrological droughts are often asynchronous or lag behind meteorological and agricultural droughts. It takes longer for precipitation to occur in water sources such as soil moisture, rivers, groundwater, and reservoir levels. Therefore, these effects are not compatible with other sectors of the economy. For example, a lack of precipitation can cause rapid soil moisture that is immediately visible to an agronomist, but the impact of a lack of water in the water does not affect water use or activities for several months.

Socioeconomic drought

Socioeconomic definitions of drought associate the supply and demand of some economic good with elements of meteorological, hydrological, and agricultural drought. It differs from the aforementioned types of drought because its occurrence depends on the time and space processes of supply and demand to identify or classify droughts. The supply of many economic goods, such as water, forage, food grains, fish, and hydroelectric power, depends on weather. Because of the natural variability of climate, water supply is ample in some years but unable to meet human and environmental needs in other years. Socioeconomic drought occurs when the demand for an economic good exceeds supply as a result of a weather-related shortfall in water supply.

CONTROLLING DROUGHT


While drought cannot be completely prevented, there are several ways to mitigate its impact and prevent it from turning into a disaster. Here are some ways to control drought:

Water Conservation: Conserving water is the most important and effective way to manage drought. It includes the efficient use of water resources, such as collecting and storing rainwater, reducing waste water, using water efficiently and promoting water conservation practices.

Sustainable Agricultural Practices:Agriculture is one of the most important users of water. Using sustainable farming practices such as crop rotation, water efficiency, and rain-resistant crops can help reduce agricultural water demand and improve soil health. Desalination: Desalination is the process of removing salt and other minerals from seawater, making it safe for human consumption. This process can help support water resources in areas affected by drought but have access to seawater.

Water Reclamation:Waste water can be reused and recycled for non-renewable purposes such as irrigation, industry and infrastructure. This will help reduce the need for fresh water and reduce the pressure on available water resources.

Groundwater Management:Groundwater is an important resource for many communities, especially in areas where water is scarce.\ Proper management, such as monitoring and controlling groundwater use, can help prevent damage and ensure sustainability.

Policy measures:The government has the ability to implement policies and regulations to manage water quality, such as water allocation policies, contingency plans, and water pricing systems. These measures can promote efficient use of water and promote conservation.

CONCLUSION


In conclusion, drought is a serious problem that can affect the environment and people's lives. It can lead to challenges such as water scarcity, food insecurity and loss of biodiversity. Drought affects developing countries where vulnerable groups may not have access to essential resources such as water and food. According to an Oxford study, an estimated 90 per cent of the world's population will be affected drought conditions by 2060. However, there are steps that can be taken to lessen the effects of dehydration. These include implementing water conservation measures, promoting sustainable agriculture, and establishing partnerships and collaborations between different stakeholders. It is also important to recognize the impact of the drought and to encourage individual and collective efforts to address it. Finally, addressing security needs to be coordinated by all segments of society, including governments, NGOs, businesses and individuals. By working together and using good strategies, we can help reduce the impact of disasters and create a longer life for all.
